Research Interests

I am interested in developing and using next generation instrumentation to better detect and characterizing exciting exoplanet systems.

Current Research Projects

(2017-Current) Precise RVs with the Habitable-zone Planet Finder

Since we installed HPF on the 10m Hobby-Eberly Telescope at McDonald Observatory in Texas, I have been actively involved in using HPF to detect and characterize new exoplanets. I am currently actively involved in maintaining and executing the HPF commissioning and the instrument team Guaranteed-Time Observations (GTO).

In recent observations of the bright M-dwarf Barnard's star, I adapted the Spectrum Radial Velocity Analyzer (SERVAL, Zechmeister et al. 2018) code to demonstrate that HPF is capable of achieving 1.53m/s radial velocity precision in the NIR. This result is currently in press in Metcalf et al. 2018 (in press).

(2016-Current) Towards Space-Quality Photometry from the Ground with Engineered Diffusers

Precision ground-based photometric facilities play a critical role in precisely characterizing transiting systems. In Stefansson et al. 2017, I led the design and implementation of an Engineered Diffuser for the ARCTIC instrument on the 3.5m Telescope at APO. Produced using specialized nanofabrication techniques, Engineered Diffusers are micro-structured optical components capable of molding the image of a star into a broad and stable top-hat shape (see video below) minimizing photometric errors due to non-uniform pixel response, atmospheric seeing effects, imperfect guiding, and telescope-induced variable aberrations seen in defocusing. Using the Engineered Diffuser on the 3.5m at APO, in our paper, we demonstrated some of the highest photometric precisions ever achieved from the ground, or 62ppm precision in 30 minute bins on 16 Cyg A—within a factor of 2 of Kepler observations of the same star.

In preparation for diffuser-assisted followup observations of TESS targets, we have used diffuser-assisted photometry to better constrain the orbital parameters of the Neptune-sized planets K2-28b and K2-100b (Stefansson et al. 2018a). In addition, in Stefansson et al. 2018b, we discussed our package iDiffuse to help plan diffuser-assisted observations.

As Engineered Diffusers are relatively inexpensive and easily scalable, I am collaborating with a number of different groups to adapt our diffuser design to other telescopes, creating a network of precision photometric followup facilities. Currently, similar diffusers have been incorporated and/or are under testing at over 10 optical and/or NIR telescopes around the world. If you are interested in trying a diffuser on your telescope, please don't hesitate to send me an email!

Lastly, our diffuser-assisted work has received some press by the Penn State Press Office.


(2015-Current) NEID: the NASA/NSF-EXPLORE Doppler spectrograph

I am part of the core instrument and science team for the NASA/NSF-EXPLORE NEID Doppler spectrograph that we are building, testing, and commissioning for the 3.5m WIYN Telescope at Kitt Peak for delivery in early/mid 2019. The design specifications of NEID are to achieve <50cm/s radial velocity (RV) precision with a goal precision of 25cm/s or better.

Similarily to HPF, to achieve these RV precision levels, exquisite temperature and pressure stability is required. Using lessons learned from the HPF Environmental Control System, we have demonstrated even better temperature stability than HPF or 0.4mK temperature stability over 30 days, to be published in Robertson et al. 2019 (submitted).

For newer updates on NEID check out the NEID research blog.

(2013-Current) Building, Testing, and Commissioning the Habitable-zone Planet Finder

I am working with Prof. Mahadevan on the Habitable-Zone Planet Finder (HPF) fiber-fed near-infrared (NIR) spectrograph we recently commissioned at the 10m Hobby-Eberly Telescope (HET) at McDonald Observatory in Texas. We have designed HPF to carry out a survey of nearby M-dwarfs to look for low-mass terrestrial exoplanets at a 1-3m/s radial-velocity (RV) precision.

1-3m/s RV precision has not been achieved in the NIR before and requires a high degree of temperature stability (sub mK) and pressure stability (<1e-5 Torr) to prevent mechanical drifts and index of refraction changes within the spectrograph. In Stefansson et al. 2016, we demonstrated that the HPF Environmental Control System is capable of maintaining sub-milliKelvin temperature stability long-term. If you are interested in downloading all of the 3D-CAD/SolidWorks models of the HPF cryostat they are freely available here and further described in our paper.

For newer updates on HPF check out the HPF research blog.

(2013) Bachelor Thesis: Observational Constraints on Dark Energy

My bachelor's thesis topic was in observational Cosmology. The project revolved around constraining the values for Dark Energy density parameter, \( \Omega_{\Lambda} \), in two ways. First, using observational evidence from SNIa explosions from Reiss et al. (1998) and Perlmutter et al. (1999). Second, performing Markov-Chain Monte-Carlo calculations using various cosmological observations to explore the likelood density projected on the \( \Omega_{\Lambda} \)- \( \Omega_{m} \) parameter space. The calculations were done on the Jötunn computer cluster at the University of Iceland.

(2012) Summer Research: Fluorescent Polymers as Integrated Light Sources

In the project we studied the use of fluorescent polymers in dielectric waveguides, but fluorescent polymers have developed rapidly in recent years, with direct applications in optical biosensing. Fluorescent polymers can induce an optical gain, enabling fabrication of optically pumped amplification and laser-sources. In the project we demonstrated the fabrication of planar waveguides, where a symmetric waveguide supporting a single guided mode was desired, which could then be directly integrated with microfluidic circuits that had been studied by the group (see project 2011).

(2011) Summer Research: Fabrication of Integrated Optical and Microfluidic Devices

I did a research project under the guidance of Dr. Kristján Leósson. The project aimed at developing a methodology for fabricating microfluidic devices on different substrates, in order to integrate them with optical circuits also studied by the Leosson Group, but PDMS microfluidic devices had not been fabricated in Iceland before. Together these integrated optical devices can be used for live cell imaging applications.
